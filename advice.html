Advice

On building the insight to choose the right path — and staying on it.

1. Maintain dual reading — academic depth and industry reality.

Academic papers tell you what is scientifically interesting. Industry media — STAT News, Endpoints News, Nature Biotechnology reviews — tells you what is commercially viable and where the real unsolved problems are. Neither alone is enough. Make both a habit early, and never let one crowd out the other.

2. Follow informed capital.

Where serious, specialized money concentrates — top biotech VCs, major pharma licensing deals, FDA breakthrough designations — is a signal from people with more information than you have. Learn to read those signals, not as gospel, but as data. When multiple top-tier investors converge on a space, something is moving there.

3. Find the rate-limiting step.

In any field you are considering, ask: what is the single bottleneck that, if solved, unlocks everything else? That is where the leverage is. LNP delivery is the rate-limiting step for nucleic acid therapeutics — the drugs exist, but getting them to the right cells efficiently is the bottleneck. Identify the equivalent in any field you evaluate. The rate-limiting step is where the power accumulates.

4. Treat every real experience as a self-knowledge experiment.

Each lab, internship, course, and project is not just work — it is data about what pulls you. The insight into which sub-field fits you does not come from reasoning in the abstract. It comes from accumulating experiences and reading them honestly. After each one, ask: what made me forget what time it was? What did I keep thinking about after I left? What felt like a slog regardless of how important it seemed? That pattern, built across years of real experiences, is more reliable than any reasoning from the outside.

5. Build a network across all three tiers — and know what each is for.

Professors give scientific depth, research direction, and the credibility that comes from their name. Senior executives and industry leaders give strategic vision and access to the field. But the most underutilized tier is people five to ten years ahead — PhD students, postdocs, early founders — who know which problems are actually hard right now, which fields are quietly stagnating, and which ones are about to move. All three matter. Most people only cultivate the top tier and miss the ground-level signal entirely.
